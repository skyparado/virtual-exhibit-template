THE OPTICAL AGE:

CDs, DVDs, and Their Limits

by Parado, Sky Hannah G. · ~12 minute read

Introduction

Magnetic storage was fast but fragile — disks warped, drives crashed, tapes degraded. In 1982, Sony and Philips answered with the Compact Disc: 650 MB on a coaster-sized disc, read by light alone, never touching the surface. It outlasted every magnetic medium of its era.

Over 30 years, optical storage evolved through three generations — CD (1982), DVD (1995), and Blu-ray (2006) — each squeezing more data onto the same disc by using shorter and shorter wavelengths of laser light.

How Optical Storage Works

A disc is a sandwich of layers. The laser reads from the bottom up:

Label & printing
Lacquer (protective coat)
Reflective aluminum layer
Polycarbonate substrate
↑ Laser reads from here

Data is stamped into the polycarbonate as a spiral of microscopic bumps called pits, separated by flat areas called lands. A laser diode shines through the base onto the track — lands reflect the beam back cleanly (high signal), while pit edges scatter it (low signal). Those highs and lows become binary data.

Why Wavelength Matters

A laser can only resolve features about half its wavelength in size. Shorter = smaller pits = more data. CD uses 780 nm (infrared), DVD uses 650 nm (red), and Blu-ray uses 405 nm (violet) — that's why capacity jumps so dramatically across formats.

Pits and Lands as Binary Data

Common misconception: pits ≠ 1s and lands ≠ 0s. On real CDs, it's the transitions between pit and land that represent a 1 — staying on either surface (no transition) is a 0. This is called NRZI encoding.

  • Noise immunity — no baseline needed between bits, easier to read at speed.
  • Self-clocking — the drive syncs its clock to the transitions themselves.

Before hitting the disc, data also goes through Eight-to-Fourteen Modulation (EFM), which converts each byte into a 14-bit pattern — guaranteeing no run of identical signal is too long or too short for the optics to track.

CD vs. DVD vs. Blu-ray

Shorter wavelength = smaller pits = more data. Same disc, totally different capacity:

Format Year Laser Wavelength Track pitch Min. pit size Capacity (1 layer)
CD 1982 Infrared 780 nm 1,600 nm 833 nm 700 MB
DVD 1995 Red 650 nm 740 nm 400 nm 4.7 GB
Blu-ray 2006 Violet 405 nm 320 nm 149 nm 25 GB

The track pitch shrinks 5× from CD to Blu-ray, giving Blu-ray ~35× more capacity on the exact same diameter disc. The tradeoff: Blu-ray's thinner protective layer (0.1 mm vs. DVD's 0.6 mm) makes it more scratch-prone.
